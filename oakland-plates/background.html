Automatic License Plate Recognition#

The huge majority of vehicles have vehicle registration plates.

The UK call these “number plates”, and the US use “license plates”.

Oakland, like many other cities in the US, and the UK, have multiple Automatic License Plate Recognition cameras, constantly feeding automated read license plates with location data to police databases.

The UK calls this Automatic Number Plate Recognition (ANPR), and the UK national police website tells us:

At present ANPR cameras nationally, submit on average around 60 million ANPR ‘read’ records to national ANPR systems daily.

The US abbreviation is ALPR.

We will concentrate on the ALPR data from the City of Oakland.

Who sees these data?#

Oakland Privacy is a:

citizens’ coalition that works regionally to defend the right to privacy and enhance public transparency and oversight regarding the use of surveillance techniques and equipment.

It has a page on who’s looking at your license plate data.

71 agencies accessed the license plate database between February and March 2021. Apart from police departments and sheriffs’ offices, these included:

Agency Count

FBI 72 US Dept Agriculture Inspector General 51 National Park Service 18 California Dept of Motor Vehicles 14 US Postal Inspection Service 4

Who should see these data?#

The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) has taken an interest in these data.

Quoting from the link below:

EFF is on a mission to use transparency as a counterbalance to mass surveillance.

The EFF analyzed the Oakland data](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2015/01/what-we-learned-oakland-raw-alpr-data). There are a number of interesting maps there. For now, let’s just note this quote from that article:

The [law-enforcement agencies refusing to release ALPR data] also argue the public interest in maintaining secrecy in ALPR data outweighs the public interest in learning how and where ALPR systems are being used.

The rub here is that law enforcement agencies like those in LA, San Diego, and Oakland aren’t using ALPR for targeted investigations, but rather running a dragnet on all drivers in their jurisdictions.

The EFF argue that we will only know if that is the case, if we can see the data.

But — what do you think? Who should be able to see the data? Should the data be public?
